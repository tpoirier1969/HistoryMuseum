Brevet Brigadier-General Charles Waite

Awarding of Medals and Honors

The rank of Brevet Brigadier-General was in part an honorary promotion recognizing gallant conduct or admirable service. The rank was difficult to receive during the war, but was commonly given at the close of the war. Over 1,000 officers were awarded this distinction during the Civil War. As the army was downsized after the war, the army had trouble placing so many top officers.

On April 2, 1865 Charles Waite of Rockland was awarded Brevet Brigadier-General U.S. Volunteers for conspicuous gallantry in the assault on Petersburg, Va.

Charles Waite entered Company A of the 27th Infantry as First Lieutenant and was commissioned and mustered on October 10, 1862. He was wounded in action at Spotsylvania, VA on May 12, 1864 and promoted to Caption of Company E and eventually to Colonel on March 6, 1865. He was mustered out on July 26, 1865. He moved to Illinois after the war and became a successful merchant and banker; he died in 1898.
