Taking Care of Veterans at Home

There were approximately 4.5 million American veterans of the 18 month U.S. participation in World War I. The average had served 12 months. Nearly half went overseas for an average of 5.5 months. Some 1.1 actually saw combat; of these 204,000 were wounded or otherwise disabled. Veterans were simply mustered out from their bases in the U.S. The government was unprepared to deal with the problems faced by returning Doughboys, especially those who were unemployed or disabled. A postwar recession brought unemployment to over 16% by 1921. That year the Veterans Bureau was established (forerunner of the Veterans Administration established in 1930). A system of hospitals was established to provide long-term care for war-related wounds and illnesses, tuberculosis caused by poison gas and mental illness caused by “shell shock.”

In both WWI and WWII an emotional disorder called Post Traumatic Stress Syndrome that develops after a traumatic event was called shell shock. Symptoms include depression, anxiety, nightmares, flashbacks and hyper-vigilance.
