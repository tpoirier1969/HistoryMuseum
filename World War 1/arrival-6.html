The Bonus Army

A war-time bonus was considered payment for the difference between what a soldier earned and what he could have earned had he not enlisted or been drafted. Before WWI the soldier’s bonus was land and money. For WWI veterans the bonus was $60.00. The American Legion was created in 1919 and led a political movement to increase the bonus. 

The proposed bonus was $1.25 for each day served overseas up to a maximum of $625.00. Those receiving smaller bonuses were paid immediately, those eligible to receive larger sums were issued certificates of service which would mature in 20 years. Veterans were allowed to borrow against their bonus. With the effects of the Great Depression of 1931, that amount was up to 50% of the bonus. Veterans organizations pressed for early payment of the full bonus. However, the federal budget and other relief programs would be at risk and the President opposed that. 

A protest was organized and veterans, some with families camped out in Washington. The Army was forced to expel the veterans from the capital. When F.D. Roosevelt came into office, he didn’t want to pay the bonus either. When public sentiment built up to pay the bonus, Congress in an election year, could not afford to look the other way. The bonus was given to veterans 10 years early. 

It is thought that the G.I. Bill of WWII was the result of the Bonus Army seeking assistance from the federal government to help them fit back into civilian life.
