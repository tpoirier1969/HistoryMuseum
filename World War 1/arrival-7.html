Armistice Day

Some of the worst fighting of World War I took place in the Flanders and Picardy regions of Northern France. The poppy was the only thing which grew in the devastated landscape. John McCrae, a Canadian doctor was moved by the sight and wrote the following poem in 1915.

Inspired by the poem, both Americans and European allies soon began selling poppies as a memorial and to raise money. Support went both to veterans and to the Franco-American Children’s League. The poppy was soon adopted by the Veterans of Foreign Wars. The poppy was assembled by disabled and needy veterans, and the VFW had the “Buddy Poppy” patented. 

In 1954, following World War II and the Korean War, Armistice Day–November 11–was officially changed to Veteran’s Day. 

In Flanders’ Fields In Flanders’ fields the poppies blow Between the crosses, row on row, That mark our place; and in the sky The larks, still bravely singing, fly Scarce heard amid the guns below. We are the dead. Short days ago We lived, felt dawn, saw sunset glow, Loved and were loved, and now we lie In Flanders’ fields. Take up our quarrel with the foe; To you from failing hands we throw The torch; be yours to hold it high, If ye break faith with us who die We shall not sleep, though poppies grow In Flanders’ field.
