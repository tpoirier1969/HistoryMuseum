Richard Mather Joplin

Richard Mather Joplin joined the Army Field Service as an ambulance driver before the U.S. had entered the war. Ambulance drivers navigated through heavy artillery fire and nighttime bombing raids bringing the wounded to field hospitals located between trenches. The following is from a letter he wrote in November of 1917. (Note: The ‘Bosche’ or ‘Boshe’ are the Germans)

AMBULANCE DRIVER
"I am now comfortably "couche" in my ambulance, where all sleeping, writing, reading is done with most of us. The car makes quite a tidy little home, with everything at one's elbow, and we Americans have had plenty of practice for them with our Pullman car berths (on the railway) .... We live very irregularly out there, having to be ready day or night for a call at a moment's notice, and snatch bits of sleep when possible. The most trying part on the nerves is driving through blackness without lights, straining one's eyes for something that may lie not 10 yards ahead on the road. Then it is a great question for judgment just how fast to run, when there are men in behind who may not survive the long ride to the hospital. But the sense of the dramatic passes quickly. After one night it becomes mere matter-of-fact business, like any fairly hazardous occupation. One thing that impresses me greatly at first was the absolute casualness of the life at the postes de secours. The men there go about their days' work as if in some frontier lumbering or mining camp; and when Boche obus begin landing a bit too close and they take to abris [shelter], it is in the same way that miners step aside to avoid a blast. The guns roaring and pounding around us become simply the noise of some gigantic industry that never ceases, and the wounded are merely those who happen to have been caught in the machinery. This was the way it appeared to me on a quiet sector of the front. We did comparatively little, so little, that I am really ashamed, and was hoping for some more duty before winter set in."
